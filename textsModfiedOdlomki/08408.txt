   "No! no!" he ejaculated hastily, "my old eyes have seen enough. These  are wizards, indeed. Let us bring them to the king. Yet if any should  wish a further proof, let _him_ stand upon the rock, that the magic  tube may speak with him."    There was a most general and hasty expression of dissent.    "Let not good magic be wasted on our poor bodies," said one; "we are  satisfied. All the witchcraft of our people cannot show the like of  this."    "It is so," remarked the old gentleman, in a tone of intense relief;  "without any doubt it is so. Listen, children of the Stars, children of  the shining Eye and the movable Teeth, who roar out in thunder, and  slay from afar. I am Infadoos, son of Kafa, once king of the Kukuana  people. This youth is Scragga."    "He nearly scragged me," murmured Good.    "Scragga, son of Twala, the great king--Twala, husband of a thousand  wives, chief and lord paramount of the Kukuanas, keeper of the great  Road, terror of his enemies, student of the Black Arts, leader of a  hundred thousand warriors, Twala the One-eyed, the Black, the Terrible."    "So," said I superciliously, "lead us then to Twala. We do not talk  with low people and underlings."    "It is well, my lords, we will lead you; but the way is long. We are  hunting three days' journey from the place of the king. But let my  lords have patience, and we will lead them."    "So be it," I said carelessly; "all time is before us, for we do not  die. We are ready, lead on. But Infadoos, and thou Scragga, beware!  Play us no monkey tricks, set for us no foxes' snares, for before your  brains of mud have thought of them we shall know and avenge.
